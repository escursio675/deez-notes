# Name of Subsidiary Books

Subsidiary Books are special books maintained to record transactions of similar nature separately instead of recording all transactions in a single journal. In large business organizations, the number of transactions is very high, and therefore it becomes difficult and inconvenient to record every transaction in one journal. To overcome this difficulty, transactions are divided according to their nature and recorded in different subsidiary books.

Subsidiary books are also known as Books of Original Entry because transactions are first recorded in these books before being posted to the ledger. The use of subsidiary books saves time, reduces errors, and makes accounting work more systematic and efficient.

The main subsidiary books maintained in accounting are as follows:

## Purchases Book

The Purchases Book records all credit purchases of goods made by the business. Only credit purchases of goods meant for resale are entered in this book. Cash purchases and purchases of assets such as furniture or machinery are not recorded here.

For example, when goods are purchased from Ram on credit, the transaction is recorded in the Purchases Book. Transactions involving credit purchases frequently appear in journal entry and ledger questions in university examinations.

## Sales Book

The Sales Book records all credit sales of goods made by the business. Only credit sales of goods intended for resale are entered in this book. Cash sales and sales of assets are not recorded here.

For example, goods sold to Hari on credit are entered in the Sales Book.

## Purchases Return Book

The Purchases Return Book, also called the Return Outward Book, records goods returned by the business to suppliers out of credit purchases. If defective or unwanted goods purchased on credit are returned, the transaction is entered in this book.

## Sales Return Book

The Sales Return Book, also called the Return Inward Book, records goods returned by customers out of credit sales. If customers return defective or damaged goods sold on credit, such transactions are recorded in this book.

Questions involving returns inward and returns outward are commonly included in trial balance and final account problems.

## Cash Book

The Cash Book records all cash and bank transactions of the business. It includes cash receipts, cash payments, cheques received, cheques issued, and bank transactions. Since it records cash transactions as well as serves as a ledger account for cash and bank, it possesses the features of both a journal and a ledger.

Different types of cash books such as Single Column Cash Book, Double Column Cash Book, and Triple Column Cash Book are frequently asked in university examinations.

## Bills Receivable Book

The Bills Receivable Book records all bills receivable accepted by debtors from whom the business is entitled to receive payment on a future date.

## Bills Payable Book

The Bills Payable Book records all bills payable accepted by the business in favor of creditors. It shows the liabilities of the business that are payable on future dates.

Bills receivable and bills payable often appear in trial balance and final account questions.

## Journal Proper

The Journal Proper is used to record those transactions which cannot be recorded in any other subsidiary book. Examples include opening entries, closing entries, adjustment entries, rectification entries, transfer entries, and purchase or sale of assets on credit.

For example, purchase of machinery on credit or adjustment entries relating to depreciation and outstanding expenses are generally recorded in the Journal Proper. Depreciation and adjustment entries are repeatedly asked in examinations.

Thus, subsidiary books help in dividing accounting work into specialized sections. They make recording of transactions easier, quicker, and more accurate. Proper understanding of the names and uses of subsidiary books is important because objective and descriptive questions on subsidiary books frequently appear in university examinations.
# Cash Book-definition, advantages, objectives

A Cash Book is a special subsidiary book in which all cash and bank transactions of a business are recorded systematically and chronologically. It records all receipts and payments made in cash as well as transactions relating to bank deposits and withdrawals. Since cash transactions occur frequently in every business, a separate book is maintained for recording them.

The Cash Book possesses the features of both a journal and a ledger. It is treated as a journal because transactions are first recorded in it, and it is also treated as a ledger because it contains cash and bank accounts. Questions regarding the meaning and types of Cash Book are frequently asked in university examinations.

All cash receipts are recorded on the debit side of the Cash Book, while all cash payments are recorded on the credit side. Similarly, bank transactions are also entered according to their nature. The balance of the Cash Book indicates the cash in hand or cash at bank available with the business.

The Cash Book is maintained because cash transactions occur almost daily and require immediate recording. Proper maintenance of the Cash Book helps the business in controlling cash and preventing fraud or misappropriation.

There are different types of Cash Book maintained according to the needs of the business. A Single Column Cash Book contains only a cash column. A Double Column Cash Book contains cash and bank columns or cash and discount columns. A Triple Column Cash Book contains cash, bank, and discount columns together. Triple Column Cash Book problems are repeatedly asked in university examinations.

Transactions such as cash sales, payment of salary, purchase of goods by cheque, cash deposited into bank, withdrawal from bank for office or personal use, rent paid by cheque, and receipt from debtors with discount are commonly recorded in the Cash Book and frequently appear in practical examination questions.

## Advantages of Cash Book

The Cash Book provides several important advantages in accounting.

One major advantage is that it records all cash and bank transactions in one place. This makes it easy to know the cash position and bank balance of the business at any time.

Another advantage is that it saves time and labour because cash transactions do not need to be posted separately into the Cash Account in the ledger. The Cash Book itself acts as the Cash Account and Bank Account.

The Cash Book also helps in reducing errors and fraud because all cash receipts and payments are recorded immediately and systematically. Proper checking of entries helps in maintaining effective internal control over cash.

Another important advantage is that it facilitates preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement. Since all bank transactions are recorded in the Cash Book, comparison with the Pass Book becomes easier. Questions relating to Bank Reconciliation Statement are frequently asked in university examinations.

The Cash Book also helps in preparing Trial Balance and final accounts because balances of cash and bank are readily available.

It further provides a complete chronological record of all cash transactions, which helps management in monitoring cash inflow and outflow effectively.

## Objectives of Cash Book

The main objective of maintaining a Cash Book is to record all cash and bank transactions systematically and accurately.

Another important objective is to know the cash balance and bank balance of the business at any point of time. This helps the business in managing liquidity and meeting financial obligations promptly.

The Cash Book is also maintained to avoid unnecessary handling of large volumes of cash transactions in the journal. By keeping a separate Cash Book, accounting work becomes simpler and more efficient.

Another objective is to maintain proper control over cash receipts and payments. Since cash is highly susceptible to misuse, systematic recording helps in preventing fraud and misappropriation.

The Cash Book also helps in preparing Bank Reconciliation Statement by comparing bank transactions recorded in the Cash Book with those recorded in the Pass Book.

It further assists in preparing financial statements because cash and bank balances obtained from the Cash Book are used directly in the Trial Balance and Balance Sheet.

Thus, the Cash Book is one of the most important subsidiary books in accounting. It ensures proper recording, control, and management of cash and bank transactions. Understanding the definition, advantages, objectives, and preparation of Cash Book is extremely important because practical questions on Triple Column Cash Book and related transactions are regularly asked in university examinations.
# Types of Cash Book

A Cash Book is a special subsidiary book used for recording all cash and bank transactions of a business. Since the volume and nature of cash transactions differ from one business to another, different types of Cash Books are maintained according to the requirements of the business. Questions regarding the different types of Cash Book are frequently asked in university examinations.

The main types of Cash Book are Single Column Cash Book, Double Column Cash Book, Triple Column Cash Book, and Petty Cash Book.

## Single Column Cash Book

A Single Column Cash Book is the simplest form of Cash Book. It contains only one amount column on each side for recording cash transactions. All cash receipts are entered on the debit side and all cash payments are entered on the credit side.

This book records only cash transactions and does not include bank or discount transactions. Since it contains only the cash column, it is suitable for small businesses where transactions are limited.

The debit side of the Single Column Cash Book records items such as cash sales, cash received from debtors, capital introduced, and rent received. The credit side records cash purchases, salary paid, wages paid, rent paid, and drawings.

The balance of the Single Column Cash Book always shows a debit balance because cash cannot be negative under normal circumstances.

## Double Column Cash Book

A Double Column Cash Book contains two amount columns on each side instead of one. It may be prepared in two forms:

### Cash and Discount Column Cash Book

In this type, one column is used for cash transactions and the other column is used for discount transactions. Discount allowed is recorded on the debit side, while discount received is recorded on the credit side.

For example, when cash is received from a debtor allowing some discount, the amount received is recorded in the cash column and the discount allowed is recorded in the discount column.

### Cash and Bank Column Cash Book

In this type, one column records cash transactions and the other records bank transactions. Transactions involving deposits into bank, withdrawals from bank, cheques received, and cheques issued are recorded here.

Contra entries arise in this type of Cash Book when cash is deposited into bank or cash is withdrawn from bank for office use. In such cases, both cash and bank accounts are affected simultaneously. Questions regarding contra entries have appeared in objective-type examination papers.

For example, when cash is deposited into bank, the Bank Account is debited and Cash Account is credited. Therefore, the transaction appears on both sides of the Cash Book.

## Triple Column Cash Book

A Triple Column Cash Book contains three amount columns on each side namely cash column, bank column, and discount column. It records cash transactions, bank transactions, and discount transactions together in one book.

This is the most complete and widely used type of Cash Book because it combines the features of both cash and bank columns along with discount columns. Triple Column Cash Book problems are repeatedly asked in university examinations.

The debit side records cash received, bank deposits, cheques received, and discount allowed. The credit side records cash payments, cheques issued, withdrawals from bank, and discount received.

Transactions frequently appearing in examination problems include cash sales, payment of salaries by cheque, cash deposited into bank, withdrawal from bank for personal use, purchase of goods by cheque, and receipt from debtors with discount.

The Triple Column Cash Book also records contra entries. Such entries are marked with the letter “C” in the ledger folio column because no separate posting to the ledger is required.

## Petty Cash Book

The Petty Cash Book is maintained for recording small and recurring expenses of the business. Examples include postage, carriage, stationery, tea expenses, conveyance, and miscellaneous expenses.

The Petty Cash Book is generally maintained under the imprest system. Under this system, the petty cashier receives a fixed amount at the beginning of a period and submits accounts of expenses incurred. The amount spent is reimbursed periodically to restore the original imprest amount.

Maintenance of a Petty Cash Book reduces the burden on the main cashier and helps in systematic recording of small expenses.

Thus, different types of Cash Books are maintained according to the size and requirements of the business. The Cash Book not only records cash and bank transactions systematically but also helps in controlling cash and preparing financial statements. Proper understanding of the types of Cash Book is extremely important because theoretical and practical questions on Single, Double, and Triple Column Cash Books are regularly asked in university examinations.
# Preparation of different types of cash books

The Cash Book is prepared to record all cash and bank transactions of a business in a systematic and chronological manner. Since businesses differ in size and nature of transactions, different types of Cash Books are maintained according to necessity. The preparation of various Cash Books is an important practical topic in accountancy and questions on preparation of Single Column, Double Column, and Triple Column Cash Books are frequently asked in university examinations.

The general format of a Cash Book contains two sides. The left side is the debit side which records cash receipts, and the right side is the credit side which records cash payments. The columns generally include Date, Particulars, Ledger Folio, and Amount.

## Preparation of Single Column Cash Book

A Single Column Cash Book contains only one amount column on each side for recording cash transactions. Only cash receipts and cash payments are recorded in this book.

The debit side records all cash received such as capital introduced, cash sales, cash received from debtors, rent received, and commission received. The credit side records all cash payments such as purchases, salary paid, wages paid, rent paid, and drawings.

The procedure for preparing a Single Column Cash Book begins with entering the opening cash balance on the debit side as “To Balance b/d.” Thereafter, all cash receipts are entered on the debit side and all cash payments are entered on the credit side according to dates.

At the end of the period, both sides are totaled. The difference between the totals is written on the credit side as “By Balance c/d” because cash balance normally remains a debit balance. On the next period, it is brought down on the debit side as “To Balance b/d.”

For example, if cash sales amount to Rs. 50,000 and salary paid is Rs. 5,000, the receipt is entered on the debit side while salary paid is entered on the credit side.

## Preparation of Double Column Cash Book

A Double Column Cash Book contains two amount columns on each side. It may consist of Cash and Discount columns or Cash and Bank columns.

### Cash and Discount Column Cash Book

In this type, one column records cash and the other records discount. Discount allowed is entered on the debit side while discount received is entered on the credit side.

For example, if Rs. 9,000 is received from a debtor in full settlement of Rs. 10,000, discount allowed of Rs. 1,000 is entered in the discount column on the debit side and Rs. 9,000 is entered in the cash column. Similar transactions involving discount allowed and discount received are repeatedly asked in examination problems.

### Cash and Bank Column Cash Book

In this type, one column records cash transactions and another records bank transactions. All cash receipts and payments are entered in the cash column, while cheque and bank transactions are entered in the bank column.

When cash is deposited into bank or withdrawn from bank for office use, contra entries arise because both cash and bank accounts are affected simultaneously. Such entries are recorded on both sides of the Cash Book and marked with the letter “C.” Questions involving contra entries have appeared repeatedly in university examinations.

For example, if Rs. 10,000 is deposited into bank, the bank column on the debit side increases while the cash column on the credit side decreases.

## Preparation of Triple Column Cash Book

A Triple Column Cash Book contains three amount columns on each side namely cash column, bank column, and discount column. It combines the features of cash transactions, bank transactions, and discount transactions into a single book.

This is the most important type of Cash Book for examination purposes because practical questions on Triple Column Cash Book are asked almost every year in university examinations.

The preparation of Triple Column Cash Book follows the same basic principles:

The opening cash balance and bank balance are entered on the debit side as “To Balance b/d.” All cash receipts, cheque receipts, and discount allowed are recorded on the debit side. All cash payments, cheque payments, and discount received are entered on the credit side.

Transactions frequently included in examination problems are cash sales, salaries paid by cheque, amount deposited into bank, withdrawal from bank for personal use, rent paid by cheque, goods purchased by cheque, and cash received from debtors allowing discount.

For example:

If cash is received from Raja Rs. 9,000 in full settlement of Rs. 10,000, discount allowed Rs. 1,000 is recorded in the discount column on the debit side and Rs. 9,000 in the cash column.

If rent is paid by cheque Rs. 10,000, the amount is entered in the bank column on the credit side.

If cash is deposited into bank, it is recorded as a contra entry affecting both cash and bank columns.

At the end of the period, cash and bank columns are balanced separately. The cash column generally shows a debit balance because cash cannot be negative, while the bank column may show either debit or credit balance depending upon bank overdraft.

## Preparation of Petty Cash Book

The Petty Cash Book is prepared for recording small daily expenses such as postage, carriage, stationery, conveyance, tea expenses, and miscellaneous expenses.

Under the imprest system, the petty cashier receives a fixed amount at the beginning of a period. Small expenses are recorded in separate columns according to their nature. At the end of the period, the amount spent is reimbursed to restore the original imprest amount.

Preparation of the Petty Cash Book helps in reducing the workload of the main cashier and ensures systematic recording of small expenses.

Thus, preparation of different types of Cash Books is an essential part of accounting practice. Proper maintenance of Cash Books helps in systematic recording of cash and bank transactions, control over cash, preparation of Trial Balance, and preparation of final accounts. Since practical questions on Cash Books are repeatedly asked in university examinations, understanding their preparation is extremely important for scoring good marks.
# Bank Reconciliation Statement

A Bank Reconciliation Statement is a statement prepared to reconcile or explain the differences between the balance shown by the Cash Book and the balance shown by the Pass Book on a particular date. In simple words, it is prepared to find out the reasons why the bank balance according to the Cash Book does not agree with the balance according to the Pass Book.

Questions regarding the meaning, preparation, advantages, and causes of differences of Bank Reconciliation Statement are frequently asked in university examinations.

The Pass Book is a copy of the customer’s account maintained by the bank. It records all deposits, withdrawals, bank charges, interest, and other transactions relating to the customer’s account. The balance shown by the Cash Book and Pass Book should theoretically agree, but in practice differences often arise because certain transactions may be recorded in one book earlier than the other.

A Bank Reconciliation Statement is generally prepared at the end of a month or at regular intervals to verify the correctness of bank transactions and locate errors or omissions.

## Causes of Difference Between Cash Book and Pass Book

There are several reasons why the balances of Cash Book and Pass Book may differ. Questions asking for causes of differences are commonly asked in examinations.

One important cause is cheques issued but not presented for payment. When a cheque is issued by the business, it is immediately recorded in the Cash Book on the credit side. However, the bank records it only when the cheque is presented by the payee for payment. Until presentation, the Pass Book balance remains different.

Another cause is cheques deposited into bank but not yet collected or credited by the bank. The business records the cheque immediately in the Cash Book, but the bank records it only after collection. This creates a temporary difference.

Bank charges debited by the bank but not recorded in the Cash Book also cause differences. The bank deducts charges directly from the account and records them in the Pass Book, while the business may record them later after receiving bank information.

Similarly, interest credited by the bank but not entered in the Cash Book causes disagreement between the two balances. Questions involving bank charges and interest credited frequently appear in examination problems.

Direct payments made by the bank on behalf of the customer, such as insurance premium or electricity bills, may also create differences if not recorded immediately in the Cash Book.

Errors committed either in the Cash Book or Pass Book are another important reason for disagreement. For example, the bank may credit or debit a wrong amount by mistake. Similar examples involving wrong credit entries have appeared in university examination questions.

## Objectives of Bank Reconciliation Statement

The main objective of preparing a Bank Reconciliation Statement is to reconcile the balances of Cash Book and Pass Book.

Another important objective is to detect errors and omissions in recording bank transactions. It helps in identifying unrecorded bank charges, direct collections, interest, and incorrect entries.

The statement also helps in checking frauds and misuse relating to bank transactions. By regular comparison of records, unauthorized transactions can be detected easily.

Preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement further helps in maintaining accuracy in accounting records and ensures reliability of cash and bank balances shown in the financial statements.

It also helps the business in knowing the actual bank balance available at a particular date.

## Procedure for Preparing Bank Reconciliation Statement

The preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement begins with the balance shown either by the Cash Book or the Pass Book.

If the starting point is the debit balance of the Cash Book, adjustments are made for all items causing differences. Cheques issued but not presented are added because they reduce the Cash Book balance but are not yet recorded in the Pass Book. Cheques deposited but not collected are deducted because they increase the Cash Book balance but are not yet reflected in the Pass Book.

Bank charges debited by the bank are deducted because they reduce the Pass Book balance but are not recorded in the Cash Book. Interest credited by the bank is added because it increases the Pass Book balance.

After making all adjustments, the resulting balance represents the balance according to the Pass Book.

For example, many university examination questions provide situations such as cheques issued but not presented, cheques deposited but not cleared, interest credited by bank, and bank charges debited. Students are required to prepare a Bank Reconciliation Statement showing the adjusted balance.

## Advantages of Bank Reconciliation Statement

The Bank Reconciliation Statement offers several advantages.

It helps in detecting errors and omissions in both Cash Book and Pass Book.

It ensures the accuracy of bank transactions recorded in the books of accounts.

It helps in locating delayed collections and unpresented cheques.

Another important advantage is that it helps in detecting frauds or unauthorized withdrawals from the bank account.

The statement also improves internal control over cash and bank transactions and increases the reliability of accounting records.

It assists management in knowing the true bank balance and maintaining proper financial discipline.

Thus, the Bank Reconciliation Statement is an important statement in accounting which helps in reconciling differences between Cash Book and Pass Book balances. Proper understanding of its meaning, causes of differences, objectives, procedure, and advantages is extremely important because theoretical and practical questions on Bank Reconciliation Statement are repeatedly asked in university examinations.
# Regions of disagreement between Cash Book with Pass Book balance

The balance shown by the Cash Book and the balance shown by the Pass Book often do not agree on a particular date. This disagreement occurs because certain transactions are recorded in one book earlier than the other or because of errors and omissions. The statement prepared to reconcile these differences is known as the Bank Reconciliation Statement. Questions regarding the causes or reasons for disagreement between Cash Book and Pass Book balances are frequently asked in university examinations.

One of the most common reasons for disagreement is cheques issued but not presented for payment. When a business issues a cheque, it immediately records the transaction in the Cash Book by crediting the bank column. However, the bank records the transaction only when the cheque is presented by the payee for payment. Until the cheque is presented, the balance shown in the Pass Book remains higher than the balance shown in the Cash Book.

Another important reason is cheques deposited into the bank but not yet collected or credited by the bank. When the business deposits a cheque, it records the amount immediately in the Cash Book. However, the bank takes some time to collect and credit the cheque. Therefore, the Cash Book shows a higher balance while the Pass Book does not yet reflect the amount.

Bank charges debited by the bank but not recorded in the Cash Book also create disagreement. The bank may deduct charges for collection, account maintenance, cheque book issue, or other services directly from the customer’s account. These deductions are recorded first in the Pass Book, while the business records them later after receiving bank information. As a result, the Pass Book balance becomes lower than the Cash Book balance.

Interest credited by the bank but not entered in the Cash Book is another reason for disagreement. Banks often credit interest on savings or deposits directly into the customer’s account. The bank records this transaction immediately in the Pass Book, but the business may record it later in the Cash Book. Therefore, the Pass Book balance becomes higher.

Similarly, direct collections made by the bank on behalf of the customer may create differences. Sometimes the bank collects dividends, interest, bills receivable, or other amounts directly and credits them to the customer’s account. These amounts appear first in the Pass Book and later in the Cash Book.

Direct payments made by the bank on behalf of the customer are also a cause of disagreement. The bank may pay insurance premium, electricity bills, loan instalments, or other obligations directly from the customer’s account. Since the business may not immediately record such payments, the Pass Book balance becomes lower than the Cash Book balance.

Dishonour of cheques or bills previously deposited also leads to disagreement. When a deposited cheque is dishonoured, the bank debits the customer’s account in the Pass Book. If the business has not yet recorded the dishonour in the Cash Book, the balances will disagree.

Errors committed either in the Cash Book or Pass Book are another important reason for disagreement. The accountant may record a wrong amount or omit a transaction in the Cash Book. Similarly, the bank may commit mistakes while recording transactions in the Pass Book. Questions involving wrong entries and errors frequently appear in examination problems.

Sometimes a customer deposits money directly into the bank account of the business without immediate information to the business. The bank records the deposit in the Pass Book, but the Cash Book remains unchanged until information is received.

Collection charges deducted by the bank while collecting cheques or bills also create differences because the net amount credited in the Pass Book may differ from the amount recorded in the Cash Book.

Timing differences between recording transactions in the Cash Book and Pass Book therefore become the major cause of disagreement between the two balances.

Thus, disagreement between Cash Book and Pass Book balances is a common feature in accounting. The main reasons include cheques issued but not presented, cheques deposited but not collected, bank charges, interest credited by bank, direct collections, direct payments, dishonoured cheques, and errors in recording. Proper understanding of these reasons is essential for preparing Bank Reconciliation Statements accurately, and such questions are repeatedly asked in university examinations.
# Preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement

A Bank Reconciliation Statement is prepared to reconcile the balance shown by the Cash Book with the balance shown by the Pass Book on a particular date. Since the balances of these two books often differ because of timing differences, omissions, and errors, a Bank Reconciliation Statement is prepared to identify the causes of disagreement and determine the correct balance.

Questions relating to the preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement are very important for university examinations and are frequently asked in practical accounting problems.

The preparation of a Bank Reconciliation Statement begins after comparing the entries in the Cash Book with those in the Pass Book. All items causing disagreement are identified and adjustments are made accordingly.

The statement may be prepared either by starting with the balance shown by the Cash Book or by starting with the balance shown by the Pass Book. The balance may be a favourable balance or an overdraft balance.

A favourable balance means that the bank balance according to the Cash Book is positive, while an overdraft means that withdrawals exceed deposits and the account shows a credit balance in the Cash Book.

## Procedure for Preparing Bank Reconciliation Statement

The first step is to ascertain the balance according to the Cash Book or Pass Book on the given date.

The second step is to identify all transactions causing disagreement between the two balances. Common causes include cheques issued but not presented, cheques deposited but not collected, bank charges, interest credited by bank, direct collections, direct payments, and errors.

The third step is to add or subtract the items according to accounting rules so as to arrive at the balance shown by the other book.

If the statement starts with the debit balance of the Cash Book, the following rules are generally followed:

Cheques issued but not presented for payment are added because they reduce the Cash Book balance but are not yet recorded in the Pass Book.

Cheques deposited but not collected are deducted because they increase the Cash Book balance but are not yet reflected in the Pass Book.

Bank charges debited by the bank are deducted because they reduce the Pass Book balance but are not yet entered in the Cash Book.

Interest credited by the bank is added because it increases the Pass Book balance but has not yet been recorded in the Cash Book.

Direct deposits made by customers into the bank account are added because they appear in the Pass Book before the Cash Book.

Direct payments made by the bank are deducted because they reduce the Pass Book balance before being entered in the Cash Book.

Dishonoured cheques are deducted because the bank reverses the earlier credit entry.

Errors and omissions are adjusted according to their nature.

Questions involving these adjustments are frequently asked in examination problems.

## Illustrative Preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement

Suppose the Cash Book shows a bank balance of Rs. 20,000 on 31st March. The following differences are identified:

Cheques issued but not presented for payment Rs. 5,000  
Cheques deposited but not collected Rs. 3,000  
Bank charges debited by bank Rs. 500  
Interest credited by bank Rs. 1,000

The Bank Reconciliation Statement will be prepared as follows:

Bank Reconciliation Statement

Balance as per Cash Book  Rs. 20,000

Add:  
Cheques issued but not presented Rs. 5,000  
Interest credited by bank Rs. 1,000

Total Rs. 26,000

Less:  
Cheques deposited but not collected Rs. 3,000  
Bank charges Rs. 500

Balance as per Pass Book Rs. 22,500

Thus, after making all adjustments, the balance according to the Pass Book becomes Rs. 22,500.

## Importance of Preparing Bank Reconciliation Statement

Preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement helps in checking the accuracy of bank transactions and maintaining proper control over cash and bank balances.

It helps in detecting errors and omissions in the Cash Book or Pass Book.

It also helps in locating unpresented cheques, uncleared cheques, and unauthorized transactions.

Another important benefit is that it assists in identifying delays in collection and recording of bank transactions.

The statement improves the reliability of accounting records and ensures that the correct bank balance is shown in the financial statements.

Regular preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement also strengthens internal control over banking operations and helps in preventing fraud and misappropriation.

Thus, the preparation of Bank Reconciliation Statement is an essential accounting procedure for reconciling differences between Cash Book and Pass Book balances. Proper understanding of its preparation, adjustment rules, and practical applications is extremely important because numerical questions on Bank Reconciliation Statement are repeatedly asked in university examinations.
