# Introduction to Depreciation Accounting – Meaning, Causes and Factors

Depreciation is an important concept in accounting which refers to the gradual and permanent decrease in the value of fixed assets due to use, passage of time, or other causes. Assets such as machinery, buildings, furniture, vehicles, and equipment are used continuously in business operations and their value reduces over time. This reduction in value is known as depreciation.

Depreciation is treated as a revenue expenditure because it represents the portion of the cost of a fixed asset consumed during an accounting period. Recording depreciation is necessary for determining the true profit of the business and showing assets at a realistic value in the Balance Sheet.

Questions relating to meaning, causes, objectives, and factors affecting depreciation are frequently asked in university examinations.

## Meaning of Depreciation

Depreciation may be defined as the systematic allocation of the cost of a fixed asset over its useful life. It is not the process of valuation of assets but the process of distributing the cost of an asset over the periods receiving benefit from its use.

For example, if machinery costing Rs. 1,00,000 is expected to be used for ten years, a part of its cost is charged as depreciation every year instead of treating the entire amount as expense in the year of purchase.

Depreciation ensures that income earned during a period is matched with the cost of assets used to generate that income.

The usual journal entry for recording depreciation is:

Depreciation A/c Dr.  
  To Asset A/c

or

Depreciation A/c Dr.  
  To Provision for Depreciation A/c

Depreciation is shown on the debit side of the Profit and Loss Account and deducted from the concerned asset in the Balance Sheet.

## Causes of Depreciation

Depreciation occurs because of several reasons.

One major cause is wear and tear. Fixed assets gradually lose efficiency due to continuous use in business operations. Machinery, vehicles, and equipment become less productive with regular usage.

Another cause is passage of time. Certain assets lose value even if they are not actively used. Leasehold property and patents reduce in value merely because time passes.

Obsolescence is another important cause. Due to technological advancement and changing market conditions, existing assets may become outdated before the end of their physical life. For example, old machines may become obsolete when improved technology becomes available.

Accidental damage may also reduce asset value. Fire, floods, earthquakes, or other unforeseen events may cause depreciation.

Depletion is another cause applicable to natural resources such as mines, oil wells, and quarries where the asset gradually reduces because of extraction.

Lack of maintenance and environmental factors such as rust, corrosion, weather conditions, and exposure to chemicals may also contribute to depreciation.

## Factors Affecting Depreciation

The amount of depreciation charged depends upon several factors.

The first factor is the cost of the asset. Depreciation is calculated based on the original cost incurred in acquiring and installing the asset.

The second factor is the estimated useful life of the asset. Assets expected to last for longer periods generally have lower annual depreciation.

The third factor is residual or scrap value. It refers to the estimated value realizable at the end of the asset’s useful life. Depreciation is charged only on the depreciable amount after deducting residual value.

Another important factor is the extent of use. Assets used more intensively generally suffer greater depreciation.

The method of depreciation adopted also affects the depreciation amount. Different methods such as Straight Line Method and Written Down Value Method result in different annual depreciation charges.

Technological development and market conditions may also influence the useful life and value of assets.

Depreciation accounting plays an important role in maintaining accurate financial records. It helps in determining correct profit, showing assets at realistic values, ensuring proper replacement planning, and complying with accounting principles.

Thus, depreciation is the gradual reduction in the value of fixed assets caused by use, time, obsolescence, and other factors. Proper understanding of its meaning, causes, and influencing factors is essential because this topic frequently appears in theoretical and practical university examination questions.

# Methods of Charging Depreciation

Depreciation is charged to allocate the cost of a fixed asset over its useful life in a systematic manner. Different methods are used depending upon the nature of the asset, business policy, and accounting requirements. Questions relating to methods of charging depreciation and calculation of depreciation are frequently asked in university examinations.

The most commonly used methods are the Straight Line Method and the Written Down Value Method. Other methods are also used in specific situations.

## Straight Line Method (Fixed Instalment Method)

Under the Straight Line Method, an equal amount of depreciation is charged every year throughout the useful life of the asset. Since the same amount is deducted annually, the value of the asset decreases uniformly.

The annual depreciation is calculated by deducting the residual or scrap value from the original cost and dividing the result by the useful life of the asset.

Formula:

$$\text{Annual Depreciation}=\frac{\text{Cost of Asset}-\text{Residual Value}}{\text{Useful Life}}$$

For example, if machinery costing Rs. 1,00,000 has a scrap value of Rs. 10,000 and useful life of 9 years:

Annual Depreciation  
= (1,00,000 − 10,000) ÷ 9  
= Rs. 10,000 per year

This method is simple to understand and calculate. It is suitable for assets whose usefulness remains almost constant over the years, such as buildings and furniture.

## Written Down Value Method (Diminishing Balance Method)

Under this method, depreciation is charged at a fixed percentage on the book value of the asset every year. Since depreciation is calculated on the reduced value each year, the amount of depreciation decreases annually.

Formula:

$$\text{Depreciation}=\text{Book Value}\times\text{Rate of Depreciation}$$

For example:

Cost of asset = Rs. 1,00,000  
Rate = 10%

First year depreciation = Rs. 10,000  
Book value = Rs. 90,000

Second year depreciation = 10% of 90,000  
= Rs. 9,000

This method is suitable for machinery, vehicles, and assets that lose efficiency rapidly in the early years.

## Annuity Method

Under the Annuity Method, depreciation is calculated considering both the cost of the asset and the interest on capital invested in the asset. This method assumes that money invested in the asset could have earned interest elsewhere.

Equal annual charges are made throughout the useful life of the asset.

This method is commonly used for leasehold assets and long-term investments.

## Sinking Fund Method

Under the Sinking Fund Method, depreciation amount is set aside every year and invested in securities so that sufficient funds become available to replace the asset at the end of its useful life.

This method emphasizes replacement planning and long-term asset management.

## Units of Production Method

Under this method, depreciation is based on actual use or output of the asset rather than time.

Formula:

$$\text{Depreciation}=\frac{\text{Cost}-\text{Residual Value}}{\text{Estimated Units}}\times\text{Units Produced}$$

This method is suitable for machinery where wear depends on production volume.

## Machine Hour Rate Method

Under this method, depreciation is charged according to the number of hours the machine is used.

Assets used more are charged higher depreciation.

This method is commonly applied in manufacturing industries.

Among all methods, the Straight Line Method and Written Down Value Method are the most important and most frequently asked in university examinations. Students should especially understand their formulas, calculations, advantages, and differences because practical questions are commonly based on these two methods.
