# Concept of Classification of Accounts

Accounts are classified into different categories in order to record business transactions systematically and accurately. The classification of accounts forms the basis of the double entry system of accounting and helps in applying the rules of debit and credit properly. According to the traditional approach, accounts are mainly divided into Personal Accounts and Impersonal Accounts. Impersonal Accounts are further classified into Real Accounts and Nominal Accounts. Questions regarding the classification of accounts and identification of account types are frequently asked in examinations.

## Personal Accounts

Personal accounts are those accounts which relate to persons, firms, companies, institutions, or representatives of persons. These accounts deal with individuals or organizations with whom the business has financial transactions. Personal accounts are classified into Natural Personal Accounts, Artificial Personal Accounts, and Representative Personal Accounts.

### Natural Personal Accounts

Natural personal accounts are the accounts of human beings. These accounts are opened in the names of individual persons with whom the business deals. Examples include Ram’s Account, Hari’s Account, Sharma’s Account, and Mohan’s Account. Such accounts record the amount payable to or receivable from these persons.

For example, when goods are sold to Ram on credit, Ram becomes a debtor of the business and a personal account is opened in his name. Similarly, when money is borrowed from Hari, Hari’s account is maintained as a creditor’s account.

### Artificial Personal Accounts

Artificial personal accounts are the accounts of institutions or entities that are recognized by law as persons although they do not possess physical existence like human beings. These include companies, banks, insurance companies, clubs, educational institutions, and government organizations. Examples are State Bank of India Account, Reliance Industries Account, Gauhati University Account, and LIC Account.

These organizations can own property, enter into contracts, sue, and be sued in their own names. Therefore, in accounting they are treated as separate legal persons.

### Representative Personal Accounts

Representative personal accounts are accounts that represent certain persons or groups of persons indirectly. These accounts generally arise due to outstanding expenses, prepaid expenses, accrued incomes, or incomes received in advance. They represent the amount due to some persons or due from some persons.

For example, Outstanding Salary Account represents salaries payable to employees, while Prepaid Insurance Account represents insurance paid in advance. Similarly, Interest Outstanding Account represents interest payable to lenders. These accounts are called representative personal accounts because they represent persons indirectly.

## Impersonal Accounts

Impersonal accounts are those accounts which do not relate directly to persons. These accounts deal with properties, assets, expenses, losses, incomes, and gains of the business. Impersonal accounts are divided into Real Accounts and Nominal Accounts.

## Real Accounts

Real accounts are the accounts of assets and properties owned by the business. These accounts continue from year to year and therefore are permanent accounts. Real accounts are classified into Tangible Real Accounts and Intangible Real Accounts.

### Tangible Real Accounts

Tangible real accounts are the accounts of assets that can be physically seen and touched. These assets possess material existence and are used in the operation of the business. Examples include Cash Account, Furniture Account, Building Account, Machinery Account, Stock Account, and Land Account.

In examination papers, accounts such as Furniture Account, Machinery Account, and Cash Account are repeatedly used in journal entries and trial balance problems.

These assets provide long-term benefit to the business and generally appear in the Balance Sheet.

### Intangible Real Accounts

Intangible real accounts are the accounts of assets that cannot be seen or touched physically but possess economic value. These assets provide rights and advantages to the business. Examples include Goodwill Account, Patent Account, Copyright Account, and Trademark Account.

Goodwill is one of the most important intangible assets and has been directly asked in objective questions in previous examinations.

Goodwill represents the reputation and earning capacity of a business. Although intangible assets do not have physical existence, they contribute significantly to business profitability.

## Nominal Accounts

Nominal accounts are the accounts relating to expenses, losses, incomes, and gains of the business. These accounts are temporary in nature and are closed at the end of the accounting period by transferring their balances to the Profit and Loss Account.

Nominal accounts are divided into Expense Accounts, Income Accounts, and Profit or Loss Accounts.

### Expense Accounts

Expense accounts record all expenses and losses incurred in carrying on business operations. These expenses reduce the profit of the business. Examples include Salary Account, Wages Account, Rent Account, Insurance Account, Commission Paid Account, and Advertising Account.

In previous university examinations, expenses such as salaries, rent, wages, commission paid, carriage inward, and insurance frequently appeared in journal entries and final accounts.

### Income Accounts

Income accounts record revenues and gains earned by the business through different activities. These accounts increase the profit of the business. Examples include Commission Received Account, Rent Received Account, Discount Received Account, Interest Received Account, and Sundry Receipts Account.

Income accounts are credited when income is earned by the business. Questions involving commission received, discount received, and rent received have appeared repeatedly in journal entry questions.

### Profit and Loss Accounts

Profit and loss accounts record gains or losses arising from business activities or special events. Examples include Profit on Sale of Assets Account, Loss by Fire Account, and Loss by Theft Account. These accounts ultimately affect the net profit or net loss of the business and are transferred to the Profit and Loss Account at the end of the accounting year.

Thus, the classification of accounts into personal, real, and nominal accounts provides a scientific and systematic framework for recording business transactions. Proper understanding of different types of accounts is essential for preparing journal entries, ledger accounts, trial balance, cash book, trading account, profit and loss account, and balance sheet, which are repeatedly asked in university examinations.
# Transaction

A transaction is the basic unit of accounting. Every business activity that involves the exchange of money or money’s worth and produces a financial effect on the business is known as a transaction. In simple words, a transaction is an event that changes the financial position of a business concern and is capable of being recorded in the books of accounts.

Accounting records only those transactions which are financial in nature. This has been repeatedly asked in previous university examinations as an objective question. Non-financial events such as appointment of employees, disputes among workers, or changes in market reputation are not recorded in accounting unless they involve money or money’s worth.

A business transaction must satisfy certain conditions before it can be recorded in the books of accounts. First, the transaction must involve at least two parties. One party gives something and the other party receives something. This principle is fundamental to the double entry system and has frequently appeared in fill-in-the-blank questions in examinations. Second, the transaction must be measurable in terms of money. Third, the transaction must affect the financial position of the business by changing assets, liabilities, capital, expenses, or revenues.

Transactions may be classified into cash transactions and credit transactions. In a cash transaction, payment is made immediately in cash or through bank. For example, purchase of furniture for cash, payment of salary, or cash sales are cash transactions. In a credit transaction, payment is postponed to a future date. For example, goods purchased from Ram on credit or goods sold to Hari on credit are credit transactions. Questions involving cash and credit transactions are very common in journal entry problems.

Transactions can also be classified into internal transactions and external transactions. External transactions take place between the business and outside parties, such as customers, suppliers, banks, or creditors. Internal transactions occur within the business itself, such as charging depreciation on machinery or creating provision for doubtful debts. Although no outsider is involved, these transactions affect the financial position of the business and therefore must be recorded.

Every transaction has a dual aspect. This means that every transaction affects at least two accounts. One account is debited and another account is credited. This principle forms the basis of the double entry system of accounting. For example, when machinery is purchased for cash, Machinery Account increases while Cash Account decreases. Thus, Machinery Account is debited and Cash Account is credited.

The following are some common examples of business transactions frequently asked in examinations:

When the owner starts business with cash, cash comes into the business and capital increases. When goods are purchased for cash, stock increases and cash decreases. When salary is paid, salary expense increases and cash decreases. When goods are sold on credit, the debtor’s account increases and sales revenue increases. When rent is received, cash increases and income increases.

Business transactions are recorded in chronological order in the journal. This point has appeared repeatedly in objective-type examination questions. After recording in the journal, the transactions are posted into ledger accounts and finally summarized through trial balance and financial statements.

Thus, a transaction is the foundation of the accounting system. Without transactions there can be no accounting records. Proper identification and classification of transactions are essential for preparing journal entries, ledger accounts, cash books, trading accounts, profit and loss accounts, and balance sheets accurately.

# Double Entry system of Book Keeping

The Double Entry System of Book Keeping is the most scientific and widely accepted system of accounting. Under this system, every business transaction affects at least two accounts, and for every debit there is a corresponding and equal credit. This system is based on the principle of dual aspect, which means that every transaction has two sides, namely the receiving aspect and the giving aspect.

The double entry system ensures complete and systematic recording of financial transactions. It has been repeatedly emphasized in university examinations that every transaction involves at least two parties and affects two accounts. Questions asking whether the double entry system is a complete system of accounting have also appeared in previous examination papers.

Under this system, one account is debited and another account is credited with an equal amount. Therefore, the total debit side always remains equal to the total credit side. This equality forms the basis of preparing the Trial Balance and helps in checking the arithmetical accuracy of accounts.

The double entry system is based on the accounting equation:

Assets = Capital + Liabilities

Every transaction changes the position of assets, liabilities, or capital, but the equality of the equation always remains maintained. This accounting equation has also appeared in objective questions in previous examinations.

For example, if the owner starts business with cash of Rs. 1,00,000, Cash Account increases and Capital Account also increases. Therefore, Cash Account is debited and Capital Account is credited. Similarly, if furniture is purchased for cash, Furniture Account increases while Cash Account decreases. Hence, Furniture Account is debited and Cash Account is credited.

The double entry system operates according to certain rules of debit and credit. These rules depend upon the type of account involved.

In case of Personal Accounts, the rule is:  
“Debit the receiver and credit the giver.”

For Real Accounts, the rule is:  
“Debit what comes in and credit what goes out.”

For Nominal Accounts, the rule is:  
“Debit all expenses and losses and credit all incomes and gains.”

Questions regarding the golden rules of debit and credit have been directly asked in previous examinations. Therefore, these rules are extremely important for examination purposes.

The process of recording transactions under the double entry system starts with journalizing. Transactions are first recorded in the Journal in chronological order. From the Journal, entries are posted to the Ledger accounts. After balancing the ledger accounts, a Trial Balance is prepared. Finally, Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, and Balance Sheet are prepared to determine the financial performance and financial position of the business.

The double entry system has several advantages. It provides complete recording of transactions because both aspects of every transaction are recorded. It helps in detecting errors through preparation of Trial Balance. It also facilitates preparation of final accounts such as Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, and Balance Sheet. Since all transactions are properly classified and recorded, it becomes easier to know the profit or loss of the business and its financial position.

Another important advantage is that it creates reliability and authenticity in accounting records. Banks, investors, creditors, and government authorities rely upon financial statements prepared under this system. The system also helps in comparison of financial results from year to year.

However, the double entry system also has some limitations. It is comparatively complex and requires proper knowledge of accounting principles. Recording every transaction with equal debit and credit requires skilled accounting work. Small businesses sometimes find it costly and time-consuming to maintain complete records under this system.

Despite these limitations, the double entry system remains the most reliable and universally accepted method of bookkeeping. Almost all modern business organizations maintain their accounts according to this system because of its accuracy, completeness, and scientific nature. Questions relating to journal entries, ledger posting, trial balance, and final accounts in university examinations are all based upon the principles of the double entry system.

# Golden rules of Debit and Credit

The Golden Rules of Debit and Credit are the basic principles used in the double entry system of bookkeeping for recording business transactions. Every financial transaction affects at least two accounts, and these rules help in deciding which account should be debited and which account should be credited. Questions relating to the golden rules of debit and credit are frequently asked in university examinations.

The rules of debit and credit are based on the classification of accounts into Personal Accounts, Real Accounts, and Nominal Accounts. Each type of account has its own rule.

## Rule for Personal Accounts

The golden rule for personal accounts is:

“Debit the Receiver and Credit the Giver.”

Personal accounts relate to persons, firms, companies, institutions, or representatives of persons. When a person receives a benefit from the business, his account is debited. When a person gives a benefit to the business, his account is credited.

For example, if goods are sold to Ram on credit, Ram receives goods from the business. Therefore, Ram’s Account is debited because he is the receiver, while Sales Account is credited.

Similarly, if cash is paid to Hari, Hari becomes the giver because he receives payment from the business. Therefore, Hari’s Account is credited and Cash Account is credited for the amount going out depending on the nature of the transaction.

Another common example asked in journal entry problems is purchase of goods from a supplier on credit. If goods are purchased from Jadu on credit, Purchases Account is debited because purchases are expenses, and Jadu’s Account is credited because Jadu is the giver of goods.

## Rule for Real Accounts

The golden rule for real accounts is:

“Debit What Comes In and Credit What Goes Out.”

Real accounts relate to assets and properties of the business. When an asset comes into the business, the asset account is debited. When an asset goes out of the business, the asset account is credited.

For example, if machinery is purchased for cash, machinery comes into the business. Therefore, Machinery Account is debited. Since cash goes out of the business, Cash Account is credited.

Similarly, when furniture is purchased for cash, Furniture Account is debited and Cash Account is credited. Questions involving machinery, furniture, and cash transactions are repeatedly asked in university examinations.

Another example is cash deposited into bank. Here, Bank Account receives cash and is therefore debited, while Cash Account is credited because cash goes out from the business cash balance.

## Rule for Nominal Accounts

The golden rule for nominal accounts is:

“Debit All Expenses and Losses and Credit All Incomes and Gains.”

Nominal accounts relate to expenses, losses, incomes, and gains of the business. Expenses and losses reduce profit and are therefore debited. Incomes and gains increase profit and are therefore credited.

For example, when salary is paid, Salary Account is debited because salary is an expense, while Cash Account is credited because cash goes out.

Similarly, when rent is received, Cash Account is debited because cash comes into the business and Rent Received Account is credited because rent received is an income.

Commission paid, wages, insurance, and advertising expenses are also debited because they are expenses of the business. On the other hand, commission received, discount received, and interest received are credited because they are incomes. Such transactions are commonly included in journal entry questions in examinations.

The application of the golden rules can be understood clearly through some important examples:

When the owner starts business with cash, Cash Account is debited because cash comes into the business, and Capital Account is credited because capital is the giver.

When goods are sold for cash, Cash Account is debited because cash comes in, and Sales Account is credited because sales generate income.

When salary is paid, Salary Account is debited because it is an expense, and Cash Account is credited because cash goes out.

When goods are purchased on credit from Ram, Purchases Account is debited because purchases are expenses, and Ram’s Account is credited because Ram is the giver.

The golden rules form the foundation of journal entries and ledger posting. Without understanding these rules, it becomes difficult to prepare journal entries correctly. Almost all practical accounting problems such as journal, cash book, ledger, trial balance, trading account, profit and loss account, and balance sheet are based on the proper application of these rules. Therefore, the golden rules of debit and credit are considered one of the most important concepts in accountancy.
# Journal- Definition, advantages

A Journal is the book of original entry or primary entry in which all business transactions are recorded for the first time in chronological order. The process of recording transactions in the journal is known as journalizing. Every transaction is entered in the journal with proper date, particulars, ledger folio, debit amount, credit amount, and narration.

The journal is called the book of original entry because transactions are first recorded in it before being posted into the ledger accounts. It provides a complete and systematic record of all business transactions in the order in which they occur. Questions such as “What is Journal?” and journal entry problems are repeatedly asked in university examinations.

Under the double entry system, every transaction recorded in the journal affects at least two accounts. One account is debited and another account is credited according to the golden rules of debit and credit. The journal therefore forms the foundation of the accounting process.

A journal entry generally contains the following parts: the date of the transaction, the account to be debited, the account to be credited, the amount involved, and a short explanation called narration. The narration explains the nature of the transaction briefly. Although narration is required in journal entries, it is not written in ledger accounts. Questions regarding narration have appeared in objective-type examinations.

Transactions are recorded in chronological order in the journal, meaning they are entered according to the sequence of dates on which they occur. This point has also been asked in examination objective questions.

The format of a journal generally consists of five columns: Date, Particulars, Ledger Folio, Debit Amount, and Credit Amount. The account to be debited is written first and the account to be credited is written below it with the word “To” before the credited account. After recording the accounts, a brief narration is written below the entry.

For example, if furniture is purchased for cash, the journal entry will be:

Furniture Account Dr.  
  To Cash Account

In this transaction, furniture comes into the business and cash goes out. Therefore, Furniture Account is debited and Cash Account is credited. Similar journal entries relating to purchase of machinery, payment of salary, commission received, goods sold, and goods purchased on credit are frequently asked in university examinations.

## Advantages of Journal

The journal provides several advantages in the accounting system.

One important advantage is that it records transactions in chronological order. This makes it easy to trace and verify transactions whenever required. Since all entries are recorded date-wise, the history of transactions can be easily understood.

Another advantage is that the journal provides a complete record of every transaction. Each transaction includes debit and credit aspects along with narration, which helps in understanding the nature of the transaction clearly.

The journal also helps in reducing errors because transactions are recorded systematically according to accounting rules. Before posting to the ledger, entries can be checked and verified properly.

It serves as legal evidence because the journal contains original records of transactions supported by vouchers, invoices, receipts, and bills. In case of disputes, journal entries may be used as proof of transactions.

The journal facilitates posting into ledger accounts. Since transactions are first classified in the journal, transferring them to ledger accounts becomes easier and more systematic.

Another important advantage is that it helps in preparation of final accounts. Proper journal entries ultimately lead to accurate ledger accounts, trial balance, trading account, profit and loss account, and balance sheet.

The journal also provides clarity and explanation of transactions through narration. This helps accountants and auditors understand the purpose and nature of each transaction without confusion.

Thus, the journal occupies a very important place in the accounting process. It acts as the starting point of the double entry system and ensures systematic, chronological, and accurate recording of all business transactions. Understanding journal and journal entries is essential because practical questions based on journalizing are frequently asked in university examinations.

# Procedure of Journalising

Journalising is the process of recording business transactions in the journal according to the rules of the double entry system. Since the journal is the book of original entry, every financial transaction is first recorded in it before posting to the ledger. Questions based on journal entries and the process of journalising are frequently asked in university examinations.

The procedure of journalising involves several systematic steps. Proper understanding of these steps is essential for preparing accurate journal entries.

The first step is to identify the transaction correctly. The accountant must read the transaction carefully and understand its nature. It is necessary to determine whether the transaction is related to purchase, sale, payment, receipt, expense, income, asset, or liability. Only transactions of financial nature are recorded in accounting. This principle has also appeared in objective examination questions.

The second step is to identify the accounts involved in the transaction. Every transaction affects at least two accounts because of the dual aspect principle of accounting. One account receives benefit while another account gives benefit. Therefore, both accounts affected by the transaction must be identified properly.

The third step is to classify the accounts involved into Personal Account, Real Account, or Nominal Account. This classification is important because the rules of debit and credit depend upon the type of account.

The fourth step is to apply the golden rules of debit and credit. For personal accounts, the rule is “Debit the receiver and credit the giver.” For real accounts, the rule is “Debit what comes in and credit what goes out.” For nominal accounts, the rule is “Debit all expenses and losses and credit all incomes and gains.” Questions regarding these golden rules are very important for examinations.

The fifth step is to determine which account should be debited and which account should be credited. The debit aspect of the transaction is recorded first, followed by the credit aspect.

The sixth step is to record the journal entry in the proper format. The date of the transaction is written in the date column. The account to be debited is written first with the abbreviation “Dr.” at the end of the account name. The credited account is written in the next line preceded by the word “To.” The amount to be debited is entered in the debit column and the amount to be credited is entered in the credit column.

The seventh step is to write narration below the journal entry. Narration is a short explanation of the transaction. It helps in understanding the purpose and nature of the transaction. Usually narration begins with the word “Being.” For example, “Being furniture purchased for cash.” Narration is an important feature of journal entries although it is not written in ledger accounts.

The following examples explain the procedure of journalising clearly.

When business is started with cash of Rs. 1,00,000, Cash Account comes into the business and Capital Account increases. Therefore, Cash Account is debited and Capital Account is credited.

Cash Account Dr.  
  To Capital Account

Being business started with cash.

When furniture is purchased for cash, furniture comes into the business while cash goes out. Therefore, Furniture Account is debited and Cash Account is credited. Similar transactions involving furniture and machinery have repeatedly appeared in examination papers.

Furniture Account Dr.  
  To Cash Account

Being furniture purchased for cash.

When salary is paid, salary is an expense and cash goes out. Therefore, Salary Account is debited and Cash Account is credited.

Salary Account Dr.  
  To Cash Account

Being salary paid.

When goods are purchased from Ram on credit, purchases increase and Ram becomes a creditor. Therefore, Purchases Account is debited and Ram’s Account is credited.

Purchases Account Dr.  
  To Ram’s Account

Being goods purchased from Ram on credit.

When rent is received, cash comes into the business and rent received is an income. Therefore, Cash Account is debited and Rent Received Account is credited.

Cash Account Dr.  
  To Rent Received Account

Being rent received.

The procedure of journalising ensures systematic, complete, and accurate recording of business transactions. It forms the basis for ledger posting, preparation of trial balance, and final accounts. Since journal entry problems are regularly asked in university examinations, proper understanding of the procedure of journalising is essential for scoring good marks in accountancy.

# Ledger, advantages, rules regarding Posting

A Ledger is the principal book of accounts in which all transactions recorded in the journal are classified and summarized under separate accounts. It is known as the book of final entry because transactions from the journal are ultimately posted into ledger accounts. Each account in the ledger contains all transactions relating to a particular person, asset, liability, expense, income, profit, or loss.

The process of transferring journal entries into ledger accounts is known as posting. After journalising, the next important step in the accounting process is posting into the ledger. Questions regarding journal and ledger and the distinction between them have frequently appeared in university examinations.

The ledger provides a complete and separate record of every account. For example, all transactions relating to cash are recorded in the Cash Account, all transactions relating to furniture are recorded in the Furniture Account, and all transactions relating to Ram are recorded in Ram’s Account. By examining a ledger account, the accountant can know the balance and position of a particular account at any time.

The ledger is usually maintained in “T” form. The left side of the account is called the debit side and the right side is called the credit side. Each side contains columns for date, particulars, journal folio, and amount.

When transactions are transferred from the journal to the ledger, the debit aspect of the journal entry is posted on the debit side of the concerned account and the credit aspect is posted on the credit side of the concerned account.

For example, if furniture is purchased for cash, the journal entry will be:

Furniture Account Dr.  
  To Cash Account

In the ledger, the Furniture Account will be debited and the Cash Account will be credited.

Similarly, transactions involving cash sales, machinery purchased, salaries paid, commission received, goods sold on credit, and purchases from creditors are commonly posted into ledger accounts in practical accounting problems.

At the end of an accounting period, ledger accounts are balanced to determine debit balances or credit balances. These balances are then used for preparing the Trial Balance and final accounts.

## Advantages of Ledger

The ledger provides several important advantages in the accounting system.

One major advantage is that it classifies all transactions under separate heads or accounts. This makes accounting records systematic and organized. Instead of searching through the entire journal, information relating to a particular account can be obtained easily from the ledger.

Another advantage is that it helps in determining the balance of each account. By balancing ledger accounts, the business can know the amount receivable from debtors, payable to creditors, available cash balance, value of assets, and amount of expenses or incomes.

The ledger also helps in preparation of Trial Balance. Since all account balances are available in the ledger, preparation of the trial balance becomes simple and accurate. Questions regarding trial balance as a statement or summary have appeared repeatedly in objective examinations.

Another important advantage is that it facilitates preparation of final accounts such as Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, and Balance Sheet. All financial statements are prepared with the help of balances obtained from the ledger.

The ledger also helps in detecting errors and omissions. Since transactions are classified properly, mistakes in recording or posting can be identified more easily.

It further provides a permanent and complete record of all business transactions relating to each account. This helps management in analyzing the financial position and performance of the business.

## Rules Regarding Posting

Posting means transferring entries from the journal to the ledger accounts. Certain rules are followed while posting transactions into the ledger.

The first rule is that every journal entry must be posted into two different accounts because every transaction has two aspects under the double entry system.

The second rule is that the account debited in the journal should also be debited in the ledger account. Similarly, the account credited in the journal should also be credited in the ledger account.

The third rule is that while posting on the debit side of an account, the name of the credited account is written in the particulars column preceded by the word “To.”

For example, if Furniture Account is debited and Cash Account is credited, the Furniture Account will contain the entry “To Cash Account.”

The fourth rule is that while posting on the credit side of an account, the name of the debited account is written in the particulars column preceded by the word “By.”

Thus, in Cash Account, the entry will appear as “By Furniture Account.”

The fifth rule is that the date of the journal entry must also be written in the ledger account while posting.

The sixth rule is that the amount recorded in the journal must be posted accurately into the corresponding ledger accounts without alteration.

The seventh rule is that after posting, the journal folio number and ledger folio number should be mentioned properly to maintain cross-reference between journal and ledger.

The eighth rule is that all ledger accounts should be balanced periodically. If the debit side total exceeds the credit side total, the account will show a debit balance. If the credit side total exceeds the debit side total, the account will show a credit balance.

Thus, the ledger occupies a central place in the accounting system. It collects, classifies, and summarizes all business transactions account-wise and forms the basis for preparing trial balance and final accounts. Proper understanding of ledger and posting is essential because practical accounting questions in university examinations are largely based on journal entries and ledger posting.


# Balancing of Ledger Accounts

Balancing of ledger accounts means determining the difference between the total of the debit side and the total of the credit side of an account. The balance of a ledger account shows the net result of transactions recorded in that account during a particular accounting period. After posting all journal entries into the ledger, each account is balanced to ascertain its closing position.

Balancing is an important step in the accounting process because the balances obtained from ledger accounts are used for preparing the Trial Balance and final accounts such as Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, and Balance Sheet. Questions relating to ledger accounts, trial balance, and final accounts are frequently asked in university examinations.

The balancing of an account is done at the end of an accounting period or at regular intervals. The totals of both the debit side and the credit side are calculated separately. If the total of the debit side exceeds the total of the credit side, the account is said to have a debit balance. On the other hand, if the total of the credit side exceeds the total of the debit side, the account is said to have a credit balance.

For example, Cash Account generally shows a debit balance because cash is an asset and assets usually have debit balances. Similarly, Furniture Account, Machinery Account, and Building Account generally show debit balances because they are assets of the business. Transactions involving furniture, machinery, and cash are commonly used in examination questions.

Capital Account and Creditors Account generally show credit balances because they represent liabilities or claims against the business. Income accounts such as Commission Received Account and Rent Received Account also usually show credit balances because incomes increase the profit of the business.

Expense accounts such as Salary Account, Wages Account, Rent Account, and Insurance Account usually show debit balances because expenses reduce the profit of the business. These accounts frequently appear in journal entries, trial balance, and final account problems in university examinations.

The procedure for balancing a ledger account involves several steps. First, all entries on both sides of the account are totaled separately. Second, the smaller side total is subtracted from the larger side total to find the difference. Third, the difference is written on the side having the smaller total as “Balance c/d” which means balance carried down. After this, both sides become equal. Finally, on the next accounting period, the balance is brought down on the opposite side as “Balance b/d” which means balance brought down.

If the debit side total is greater, the balancing figure is written on the credit side as “Balance c/d.” This balance will then appear on the debit side of the next period as “Balance b/d.” Similarly, if the credit side total is greater, the balancing figure is written on the debit side and carried forward to the credit side of the next period.

For example, suppose Cash Account has total receipts of Rs. 50,000 on the debit side and total payments of Rs. 35,000 on the credit side. The difference of Rs. 15,000 represents cash in hand and is written on the credit side as “Balance c/d.” In the next accounting period, it appears on the debit side as “Balance b/d.”

Certain accounts may not require balancing. Nominal accounts such as Salary Account, Rent Account, and Commission Account are usually transferred to the Profit and Loss Account at the end of the accounting year instead of being carried forward permanently. Real and personal accounts generally continue from year to year and therefore their balances are carried forward.

Balancing of ledger accounts serves several important purposes. It helps in determining the exact position of every account. It provides the figures necessary for preparing the Trial Balance. It also helps in detecting errors because incorrect postings may disturb the balances of accounts. Furthermore, balancing provides information regarding assets, liabilities, incomes, expenses, debtors, creditors, and cash position of the business.

Thus, balancing of ledger accounts is an essential part of the accounting process. It summarizes the effect of all transactions relating to a particular account and provides the basis for preparing accurate financial statements. Proper understanding of balancing is very important because practical accounting problems in examinations frequently require balancing of ledger accounts before preparing trial balance and final accounts.

# Trial Balance- Definition, objectives, procedure of preparation

A Trial Balance is a statement prepared at a particular date showing the balances of all ledger accounts. It contains the debit balances in one column and the credit balances in another column. The main purpose of preparing a trial balance is to check the arithmetical accuracy of the books of accounts maintained under the double entry system.

Questions asking whether trial balance is a statement, summary, or account have repeatedly appeared in university examinations. The correct answer is that a trial balance is a statement.

A trial balance is prepared after balancing all ledger accounts. Since every transaction under the double entry system has equal debit and credit aspects, the total of debit balances and the total of credit balances in the trial balance should agree. If both totals are equal, it indicates that the books are arithmetically correct to a certain extent.

The trial balance is usually prepared at the end of an accounting period before preparing final accounts such as Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, and Balance Sheet. Questions involving preparation of final accounts from a trial balance are frequently asked in university examinations.

The trial balance includes balances of assets, liabilities, capital, expenses, incomes, gains, and losses. Assets and expenses generally appear on the debit side, while liabilities, capital, and incomes generally appear on the credit side.

For example, Cash Account, Furniture Account, Machinery Account, Purchases Account, Salary Account, and Wages Account generally show debit balances. On the other hand, Capital Account, Creditors Account, Sales Account, Bills Payable Account, and Commission Received Account generally show credit balances. These items frequently appear in examination trial balance questions.

## Objectives of Trial Balance

The primary objective of preparing a trial balance is to check the arithmetical accuracy of ledger posting. If the total debit balances and total credit balances agree, it indicates that postings and calculations are mathematically correct.

Another important objective is to facilitate the preparation of final accounts. The trial balance provides summarized balances of all accounts which are directly used for preparing Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, and Balance Sheet.

The trial balance also helps in locating errors. If the totals of debit and credit columns do not agree, it indicates that some errors have occurred in journalizing, posting, balancing, or totaling.

Another objective is to provide a summary of all ledger accounts in a single statement. This helps the accountant and management to understand the financial position of the business easily.

The trial balance further assists in preparing adjustment entries and rectification entries. It acts as a basis for preparing financial statements and evaluating business performance.

## Procedure of Preparation of Trial Balance

The preparation of a trial balance involves a systematic procedure.

The first step is to prepare and balance all ledger accounts. Every account in the ledger must be totaled and balanced properly before preparing the trial balance.

The second step is to list the names of all ledger accounts in a statement. Usually, accounts are arranged in a particular order such as assets, liabilities, capital, expenses, and incomes.

The third step is to determine the balance of each account. Accounts having debit balances are entered in the debit column, while accounts having credit balances are entered in the credit column.

The fourth step is to record the amount of each balance in the appropriate column. Care must be taken to place debit balances only in the debit column and credit balances only in the credit column.

The fifth step is to total both the debit and credit columns separately. If the totals agree, the trial balance is said to be balanced.

For example, Cash Account, Furniture Account, Machinery Account, Purchases Account, Wages Account, and Debtors Account usually appear on the debit side of the trial balance. Capital Account, Creditors Account, Sales Account, Bills Payable Account, and Sundry Receipts Account generally appear on the credit side. These items are commonly used in university examination problems relating to preparation of final accounts.

There are mainly three methods of preparing a trial balance. Under the Balance Method, only balances of ledger accounts are taken. Under the Total Method, totals of debit and credit sides of ledger accounts are taken. Under the Total and Balance Method, both totals and balances are shown together. Among these methods, the Balance Method is the most commonly used in practice.

Although agreement of trial balance indicates arithmetical accuracy, it does not guarantee complete correctness of accounts. Certain errors such as errors of omission, errors of principle, and compensating errors may remain undetected even if the trial balance agrees.

Thus, the trial balance is an important statement in the accounting process. It acts as a bridge between ledger accounts and final accounts and helps in checking accuracy, summarizing accounts, and preparing financial statements. Proper understanding of trial balance is essential because questions based on its preparation and use are frequently asked in university examinations.
