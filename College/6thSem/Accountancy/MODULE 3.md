# Final Account

Final Accounts are the accounts prepared at the end of an accounting period to ascertain the profit or loss of a business and to know its financial position. They represent the final stage of the accounting process and are prepared from the Trial Balance after making necessary adjustments. Final Accounts mainly consist of the Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, and Balance Sheet.

Questions relating to preparation of Final Accounts from a Trial Balance with adjustments are among the most important practical questions in university examinations.

The main objective of preparing Final Accounts is to determine whether the business has earned profit or suffered loss during the accounting period and to show the financial position of the business on a particular date.

Final Accounts are usually prepared at the end of the accounting year after preparing the Trial Balance and passing adjustment entries relating to outstanding expenses, prepaid expenses, depreciation, accrued incomes, income received in advance, bad debts, and closing stock. Questions involving adjustments such as depreciation and outstanding expenses are frequently asked in examinations.

The Final Accounts include three important statements.

## Trading Account

The Trading Account is prepared to ascertain the gross profit or gross loss arising from buying and selling of goods during an accounting period. It records direct expenses and direct revenues related to trading activities.

The debit side of the Trading Account generally contains opening stock, purchases, carriage inward, wages, customs duty, and other direct expenses. The credit side contains sales and closing stock.

If the credit side exceeds the debit side, the result is gross profit. If the debit side exceeds the credit side, the result is gross loss.

Gross Profit is transferred to the Profit and Loss Account.

Transactions involving purchases, sales, returns inward, returns outward, carriage inward, and closing stock frequently appear in Trading Account problems in university examinations.

## Profit and Loss Account

The Profit and Loss Account is prepared to ascertain the net profit or net loss of the business during the accounting period. It includes all indirect expenses and indirect incomes.

The debit side of the Profit and Loss Account contains expenses and losses such as salary, rent, insurance, carriage outward, depreciation, bad debts, office expenses, and advertising expenses. The credit side contains gross profit brought down and indirect incomes such as commission received, discount received, rent received, and interest received.

If the credit side exceeds the debit side, the result is net profit. If the debit side exceeds the credit side, the result is net loss.

Net profit increases the capital of the owner, while net loss decreases it.

Questions involving depreciation, bad debts, outstanding expenses, prepaid expenses, and accrued incomes are commonly included in Profit and Loss Account problems.

## Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet is a statement prepared on a particular date showing the financial position of the business. It contains assets on one side and liabilities along with capital on the other side.

The Balance Sheet is not an account but a statement. This point has frequently appeared in objective examination questions.

Assets include cash, bank balance, furniture, machinery, building, stock, debtors, bills receivable, and prepaid expenses. Liabilities include creditors, bills payable, outstanding expenses, bank overdraft, and loans.

Capital is adjusted by adding net profit and deducting drawings and net loss.

The total of assets must always be equal to the total of liabilities and capital because of the accounting equation:

\text{Assets} = \text{Capital} + \text{Liabilities}

Questions relating to Balance Sheet preparation and classification of assets and liabilities are repeatedly asked in university examinations.

## Objectives of Final Accounts

The primary objective of Final Accounts is to determine the profitability of the business through Trading Account and Profit and Loss Account.

Another important objective is to determine the financial position of the business through the Balance Sheet.

Final Accounts also help owners, investors, creditors, banks, and management in decision-making and evaluating business performance.

They provide information regarding sales, purchases, expenses, incomes, assets, liabilities, and capital of the business.

Final Accounts further help in comparing the financial performance of the business from one year to another.

## Importance of Final Accounts

Final Accounts are essential because they provide summarized financial information in a systematic manner.

They help in determining gross profit, net profit, and financial position accurately.

They also help in calculating tax liabilities and fulfilling legal requirements.

Banks and financial institutions rely on Final Accounts while granting loans and credit facilities.

Investors and creditors use Final Accounts to judge the profitability and stability of the business.

Thus, Final Accounts form the concluding stage of the accounting process. They summarize all business transactions and reveal the operational results and financial condition of the business. Proper understanding of Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, Balance Sheet, and related adjustments is extremely important because practical questions on Final Accounts are among the most frequently asked questions in university examinations.

# Preparation of Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account with adjustments

The Trading Account and Profit and Loss Account are prepared at the end of an accounting period to determine the gross profit or gross loss and the net profit or net loss of a business. These accounts are prepared from the Trial Balance after incorporating necessary adjustments. Questions involving preparation of Trading Account and Profit and Loss Account with adjustments are among the most important practical questions in university examinations.

The Trading Account is prepared first to ascertain gross profit or gross loss arising from buying and selling of goods. After calculating gross profit or gross loss, the Profit and Loss Account is prepared to determine net profit or net loss after considering indirect expenses and incomes.

Adjustments are necessary because certain expenses and incomes may relate partly to the current accounting period and partly to future periods. Similarly, some expenses or incomes may remain unpaid or unreceived at the end of the year. Therefore, proper adjustments are required to show the correct profit and financial position.

## Preparation of Trading Account

The Trading Account contains direct expenses and direct revenues relating to goods dealt in by the business.

The debit side of the Trading Account generally includes opening stock, net purchases, carriage inward, wages, freight, import duty, and other direct expenses.

Net purchases are calculated by deducting purchase returns from total purchases.

The credit side contains net sales and closing stock.

Net sales are obtained after deducting sales returns from total sales.

The difference between the two sides represents gross profit or gross loss.

If the credit side exceeds the debit side, the result is gross profit.

If the debit side exceeds the credit side, the result is gross loss.

Gross profit is transferred to the credit side of the Profit and Loss Account, while gross loss is transferred to the debit side.

Questions involving purchases, sales, carriage inward, returns inward, returns outward, and closing stock are repeatedly asked in examination problems.

## Preparation of Profit and Loss Account

The Profit and Loss Account records all indirect expenses and indirect incomes.

The debit side includes salaries, rent, insurance, carriage outward, bad debts, depreciation, office expenses, advertising expenses, discount allowed, and other indirect expenses.

The credit side includes gross profit transferred from the Trading Account along with incomes such as commission received, rent received, discount received, and interest received.

The difference between the two sides represents net profit or net loss.

If the credit side exceeds the debit side, the result is net profit.

If the debit side exceeds the credit side, the result is net loss.

Net profit is added to capital, while net loss is deducted from capital in the Balance Sheet.

## Adjustments in Final Accounts

Adjustments are very important in preparation of final accounts. Questions involving adjustments are frequently asked in university examinations.

### Closing Stock

Closing stock represents unsold goods remaining at the end of the accounting period.

It appears on the credit side of the Trading Account and also on the assets side of the Balance Sheet.

### Outstanding Expenses

Outstanding expenses are expenses due but not yet paid.

Such expenses are added to the respective expense in the Profit and Loss Account and shown as liabilities in the Balance Sheet.

For example, outstanding salary is added to salary expense and shown as Outstanding Salary under liabilities.

### Prepaid Expenses

Prepaid expenses are expenses paid in advance relating to future periods.

These are deducted from the related expense in the Profit and Loss Account and shown as assets in the Balance Sheet.

For example, prepaid insurance is deducted from Insurance Account and shown as an asset.

### Accrued Income

Accrued income is income earned but not yet received.

It is added to the related income in the Profit and Loss Account and shown as an asset in the Balance Sheet.

### Income Received in Advance

Income received in advance relates to future accounting periods.

It is deducted from the related income in the Profit and Loss Account and shown as a liability in the Balance Sheet.

### Depreciation

Depreciation represents reduction in value of fixed assets due to wear and tear or passage of time.

It is shown on the debit side of the Profit and Loss Account and deducted from the concerned asset in the Balance Sheet.

Questions involving depreciation on machinery, furniture, and building are frequently asked in examinations.

### Bad Debts

Bad debts represent debts which become irrecoverable.

They are shown on the debit side of the Profit and Loss Account and deducted from debtors in the Balance Sheet.

### Provision for Doubtful Debts

Provision for doubtful debts is created to cover possible future bad debts.

It is shown on the debit side of the Profit and Loss Account and deducted from debtors in the Balance Sheet.

## Important Points Regarding Adjustments

Every adjustment generally affects two accounts. Therefore, it appears in two places in the final accounts.

For example, closing stock appears in the Trading Account and Balance Sheet. Outstanding salary appears in the Profit and Loss Account and Balance Sheet. Depreciation appears in the Profit and Loss Account and reduces the asset value in the Balance Sheet.

This dual effect principle is very important for examination purposes.

## Illustrative Structure

The Trading Account is prepared first:

Opening Stock + Purchases + Direct Expenses are shown on the debit side.

Sales + Closing Stock are shown on the credit side.

The resulting balance gives Gross Profit or Gross Loss.

Then the Profit and Loss Account is prepared:

Indirect Expenses and Losses are shown on the debit side.

Gross Profit and Incomes are shown on the credit side.

The resulting balance gives Net Profit or Net Loss.

## Importance of Trading Account and Profit and Loss Account

These accounts help in determining the profitability and efficiency of business operations.

They help management in controlling expenses and improving profitability.

They also provide information to investors, creditors, banks, and tax authorities regarding business performance.

Preparation of final accounts with proper adjustments ensures that revenues and expenses are matched correctly according to accounting principles.

Thus, preparation of Trading Account and Profit and Loss Account with adjustments is an essential part of accounting. Proper understanding of adjustments such as closing stock, depreciation, outstanding expenses, prepaid expenses, accrued income, and bad debts is extremely important because practical questions based on these adjustments are repeatedly asked in university examinations.
