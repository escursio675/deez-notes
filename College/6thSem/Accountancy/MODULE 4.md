# Concept of Capital Expenditure and Revenue Expenditure

Expenditure refers to the amount spent by a business for acquiring assets, earning income, or carrying on day-to-day operations. In accounting, expenditure is broadly classified into Capital Expenditure and Revenue Expenditure. Proper classification of expenditure is important because it directly affects the profit of the business and the preparation of financial statements. Questions asking students to distinguish between capital and revenue expenditure or classify transactions under these categories are frequently asked in university examinations.

## Capital Expenditure

Capital Expenditure is the expenditure incurred for acquiring fixed assets, increasing the earning capacity of the business, or obtaining benefits for more than one accounting period. Such expenditure is not incurred for regular business operations but for improving or expanding the business structure.

Capital expenditure results in the acquisition of an asset or an increase in the value or useful life of an existing asset. Since the benefit of such expenditure extends over several years, it is shown in the Balance Sheet as an asset and not charged fully to the current year’s Profit and Loss Account.

Examples of capital expenditure include purchase of land, building, machinery, furniture, installation charges of machinery, cost of transport for bringing machinery, legal charges for acquiring property, major improvements to fixed assets, and expenses incurred to make a newly purchased asset ready for use.

For example, if a business purchases machinery for Rs. 2,00,000 and spends Rs. 20,000 on installation, the installation cost is treated as capital expenditure because it is necessary to bring the machinery into working condition.

Capital expenditure generally creates long-term benefits, increases production capacity, extends asset life, or improves operational efficiency.

## Revenue Expenditure

Revenue Expenditure is the expenditure incurred for carrying out the normal day-to-day operations of the business and earning revenue during the current accounting period. The benefit of such expenditure is consumed within one accounting year and therefore it is charged fully to the Trading Account or Profit and Loss Account.

Revenue expenditure does not create a new asset and does not increase the earning capacity of the business permanently. It is recurring in nature and is incurred regularly to maintain the business.

Examples of revenue expenditure include purchase of goods for resale, wages, salaries, rent, carriage inward, carriage outward, repairs and maintenance, insurance, electricity charges, office expenses, advertising, and depreciation.

For example, salary paid to employees and repair expenses for maintaining machinery are treated as revenue expenditure because their benefits are exhausted during the current period.

Revenue expenditure helps the business continue normal operations and maintain existing assets rather than creating new assets.

## Difference Between Capital Expenditure and Revenue Expenditure

Capital expenditure is incurred to acquire or improve fixed assets and provides benefits for more than one accounting period, whereas revenue expenditure is incurred for daily business activities and its benefit is exhausted within the current accounting period.

Capital expenditure appears in the Balance Sheet as an asset, while revenue expenditure appears in the Trading Account or Profit and Loss Account as an expense.

Capital expenditure is generally non-recurring and long-term in nature, whereas revenue expenditure is recurring and short-term in nature.

Examples of capital expenditure include purchase of machinery and building construction, while examples of revenue expenditure include salary paid, repairs, and office expenses.

Certain expenditures require careful judgment. For example, ordinary repair of machinery is revenue expenditure, but major repairs that increase the useful life of machinery are treated as capital expenditure.

Thus, proper distinction between capital expenditure and revenue expenditure is essential in accounting because incorrect classification may lead to incorrect calculation of profit and inaccurate presentation of the financial position of the business. Understanding this concept is extremely important because theoretical and practical questions on classification of expenditure frequently appear in university examinations.

# Concept of Bad Debts

Bad Debts refer to the amount owed by customers or debtors which becomes irrecoverable and cannot be collected by the business. In the ordinary course of business, goods are often sold on credit with the expectation that customers will pay in the future. However, sometimes certain debtors fail to make payment due to insolvency, bankruptcy, disappearance, financial difficulties, or refusal to pay. Such unrecoverable amounts are treated as bad debts.

Bad debts represent a loss to the business because goods have already been delivered or services have already been provided but the corresponding amount cannot be realized. Therefore, bad debts are regarded as a loss and are recorded as an expense in the books of accounts.

Questions on bad debts, their accounting treatment, adjustment entries, and presentation in final accounts are frequently asked in university examinations.

When a debt is confirmed as irrecoverable, the amount is written off from the books by passing the following journal entry:

Bad Debts A/c Dr.  
  To Debtor’s A/c

In this entry, the Bad Debts Account is debited because bad debt is considered a loss, and the Debtor’s Account is credited because the amount receivable from the customer is cancelled.

For example, suppose goods worth Rs. 10,000 were sold to Rahul on credit and later Rahul becomes insolvent and only Rs. 2,000 can be recovered. The remaining Rs. 8,000 becomes bad debt and is written off as a loss.

Bad debts generally arise because of reasons such as bankruptcy of customers, inability to trace debtors, disputes regarding payment, unexpected business failures, or prolonged non-payment.

In final accounts, bad debts are treated as an indirect expense. They are shown on the debit side of the Profit and Loss Account because they reduce the profit of the business. At the same time, the amount of bad debts is deducted from Sundry Debtors in the Balance Sheet to show the realizable value of debtors.

Sometimes, businesses expect that some debtors may become irrecoverable in the future even though actual bad debts have not yet occurred. In such cases, a Provision for Doubtful Debts is created. Provision is made on the basis of estimated future losses and follows the Conservatism Principle of accounting, which requires anticipated losses to be recognized.

It is important to distinguish between bad debts and doubtful debts. Bad debts are amounts definitely known to be irrecoverable, whereas doubtful debts are amounts that may or may not become irrecoverable in the future.

Bad debts play an important role in determining the true profit of the business because failure to record them would overstate both profit and assets. Therefore, proper treatment of bad debts ensures that financial statements present a true and fair view of the financial position of the business.

Thus, bad debts represent actual losses arising from non-recovery of amounts due from customers and form an important adjustment while preparing Profit and Loss Account and Balance Sheet.

# Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts

Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts is an estimated amount set aside by a business to cover possible losses that may arise due to non-recovery of amounts from debtors in the future. In business, goods are frequently sold on credit and there is always a possibility that some customers may fail to pay. Since the exact amount of future bad debts cannot be known in advance, an estimated provision is created to ensure that profits are not overstated and assets are not shown at an unrealistically high value.

Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts is created according to the Conservatism Principle of accounting, which states that anticipated losses should be recognized but anticipated gains should not be recorded.

Questions relating to provision for bad and doubtful debts, journal entries, adjustment treatment, and presentation in final accounts are commonly asked in university examinations.

Unlike bad debts, which represent actual losses already identified, provision for bad and doubtful debts represents expected future losses. Therefore, provision is created before the actual loss occurs.

The journal entry for creating provision is:

Profit and Loss A/c Dr.  
  To Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts A/c

In this entry, Profit and Loss Account is debited because provision is treated as an expense, and Provision Account is credited because it represents an anticipated reduction in realizable debtors.

For example, suppose debtors amount to Rs. 1,00,000 and the business decides to create a provision at 5%.

Provision =  
5% of Rs. 1,00,000 = Rs. 5,000

Journal Entry:

Profit and Loss A/c Dr. Rs. 5,000  
  To Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts A/c Rs. 5,000

When preparing final accounts, provision is treated in two places:

In the Profit and Loss Account, the amount of provision is shown on the debit side as an expense.

In the Balance Sheet, the provision amount is deducted from Sundry Debtors to show the estimated realizable value.

Example:

Sundry Debtors = Rs. 1,00,000  
Less: Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts = Rs. 5,000

Net Debtors = Rs. 95,000

When an old provision already exists and a new provision has to be created, only the increase or decrease is adjusted through the Profit and Loss Account.

For example:

Old Provision = Rs. 4,000  
New Provision Required = Rs. 6,000

Additional provision = Rs. 2,000

Entry:

Profit and Loss A/c Dr. Rs. 2,000  
  To Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts A/c Rs. 2,000

If the new provision required is lower than the existing provision, the excess amount is transferred back to Profit and Loss Account.

It is important to distinguish between bad debts and provision for bad and doubtful debts. Bad debts represent actual irrecoverable amounts and are written off immediately, whereas provision represents estimated future losses and is created in advance.

Provision for bad and doubtful debts helps in determining the true profit of the business and presents debtors at a realistic value in the Balance Sheet.

# Provision for Discount on Debtors

Provision for Discount on Debtors is the estimated amount set aside by a business to allow future cash discounts to debtors for prompt payment. In business, customers who make early payments are often allowed a discount as an incentive. Since some discount is expected to be given in future, an estimate is made in advance and charged to the current year’s Profit and Loss Account.

Provision for Discount on Debtors follows the Matching Principle of accounting because the expected discount relating to current credit sales is recognized in the same accounting period. It is also related to the Conservatism Principle, which aims at showing realistic profit.

Questions on provision for discount on debtors and its treatment in final accounts are frequently asked in university examinations.

Provision for discount is created only on good debtors. Therefore, before calculating provision for discount, bad debts and provision for bad and doubtful debts must first be deducted from total debtors.

The journal entry for creating provision is:

Profit and Loss A/c Dr.  
  To Provision for Discount on Debtors A/c

In this entry, Profit and Loss Account is debited because expected discount is treated as an expense, and Provision for Discount Account is credited.

For example, suppose:

Sundry Debtors = Rs. 1,00,000  
Bad Debts = Rs. 5,000  
Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts = Rs. 10,000  
Provision for Discount = 2%

Calculation:

Debtors = Rs. 1,00,000  
Less: Bad Debts = Rs. 5,000  
Balance = Rs. 95,000

Less: Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts = Rs. 10,000  
Good Debtors = Rs. 85,000

Provision for Discount = 2% of Rs. 85,000  
= Rs. 1,700

Thus, provision is calculated only after reducing bad debts and doubtful debts.

In final accounts, Provision for Discount on Debtors appears in two places.

In the Profit and Loss Account, it is shown on the debit side as an expense.

In the Balance Sheet, it is deducted from Sundry Debtors after deducting bad debts and provision for doubtful debts.

Presentation:

Sundry Debtors ........ Rs. 1,00,000  
Less: Bad Debts ........ Rs. 5,000  
Less: Provision for Bad and Doubtful Debts ........ Rs. 10,000  
Less: Provision for Discount on Debtors ........ Rs. 1,700

Net Debtors ........ Rs. 83,300

If an old provision already exists, only the increase or decrease is adjusted through the Profit and Loss Account.

Provision for Discount on Debtors should not be confused with discount allowed. Discount allowed is actual discount already given and recorded, whereas provision for discount is an estimated future discount.

Thus, Provision for Discount on Debtors is created to estimate future discounts that may be allowed to debtors and helps in presenting debtors at their expected realizable value.

# Outstanding Expenses

Outstanding Expenses are those expenses which have been incurred during the current accounting period but have not yet been paid by the end of the accounting year. In other words, these expenses belong to the current year but their payment remains due. Such expenses are also known as accrued expenses or unpaid expenses.

According to the Matching Principle of accounting, expenses must be recorded in the same period in which the related benefit is received, irrespective of whether payment has actually been made. Therefore, outstanding expenses are recognized in the current year’s accounts to determine the correct profit or loss of the business.

Questions regarding outstanding expenses, adjustment entries, and treatment in final accounts are frequently asked in university examinations.

Examples of outstanding expenses include unpaid salary, unpaid rent, outstanding wages, unpaid electricity charges, and unpaid interest.

For example, suppose salary paid during the year amounts to Rs. 50,000, but salary of Rs. 5,000 for the current year remains unpaid at year-end. Since this unpaid amount relates to the current accounting period, it must be included as an expense of the current year.

The journal entry for recording outstanding expenses is:

Expense A/c Dr.  
  To Outstanding Expense A/c

For example:

Salary A/c Dr.  
  To Outstanding Salary A/c

In this entry, the expense account is debited because expenses increase, and the Outstanding Expense Account is credited because it represents an amount payable and therefore becomes a liability.

Outstanding expenses affect final accounts in two places.

In the Profit and Loss Account, the outstanding amount is added to the related expense because it belongs to the current accounting period.

In the Balance Sheet, the outstanding expense is shown on the liabilities side because payment is still due.

Example:

Salary paid during the year = Rs. 50,000  
Outstanding salary = Rs. 5,000

In Profit and Loss Account:

Salary = Rs. 55,000

In Balance Sheet:

Outstanding Salary = Rs. 5,000 (Liability)

This dual effect of adjustment is an important examination point.

Outstanding expenses increase the total expenses of the business and therefore reduce net profit.

It is important to distinguish outstanding expenses from prepaid expenses. Outstanding expenses are expenses due but unpaid and are treated as liabilities, whereas prepaid expenses are expenses paid in advance and are treated as assets.

Thus, Outstanding Expenses represent expenses incurred but not yet paid at the end of the accounting period. Proper adjustment of such expenses ensures correct calculation of profit and true presentation of the financial position of the business.

# Prepaid Expenses

Prepaid Expenses are those expenses which have been paid in advance during the current accounting period but relate partly or wholly to future accounting periods. In other words, they represent payments made before the benefit from the expense has actually been received.

According to the Matching Principle of accounting, only those expenses which relate to the current accounting period should be charged to the current year’s Profit and Loss Account. Therefore, the portion of expense paid in advance must be excluded from the current year’s expense and carried forward to the next period.

Questions regarding prepaid expenses, adjustment entries, and their treatment in final accounts are frequently asked in university examinations.

Examples of prepaid expenses include prepaid insurance, prepaid rent, prepaid salary, prepaid advertisement expenses, and prepaid subscriptions.

For example, suppose insurance of Rs. 12,000 is paid for twelve months and at the end of the accounting year, Rs. 3,000 relates to the next year. Since this amount does not belong to the current year, it is treated as prepaid expense.

The journal entry for recording prepaid expenses is:

Prepaid Expense A/c Dr.  
  To Expense A/c

For example:

Prepaid Insurance A/c Dr.  
  To Insurance A/c

In this entry, Prepaid Expense Account is debited because it represents future economic benefit and therefore becomes an asset. The Expense Account is credited to reduce the expense relating to the current year.

Prepaid expenses affect final accounts in two places.

In the Profit and Loss Account, prepaid expenses are deducted from the related expense because that portion belongs to future periods.

In the Balance Sheet, prepaid expenses are shown on the assets side because they represent benefits yet to be received.

Example:

Insurance paid = Rs. 12,000  
Prepaid insurance = Rs. 3,000

In Profit and Loss Account:

Insurance Expense = Rs. 9,000

In Balance Sheet:

Prepaid Insurance = Rs. 3,000 (Asset)

This dual effect of adjustment is an important examination point.

Prepaid expenses reduce the expenses charged to the current year and therefore increase net profit.

It is important to distinguish prepaid expenses from outstanding expenses. Prepaid expenses are expenses paid in advance and shown as assets, whereas outstanding expenses are expenses due but unpaid and shown as liabilities.

Thus, Prepaid Expenses represent advance payments for future benefits and are adjusted to ensure that only current year expenses are charged to the Profit and Loss Account. Proper treatment of prepaid expenses helps in presenting the correct profit and true financial position of the business.

# Accrued Income

Accrued Income refers to income which has been earned during the current accounting period but has not yet been received by the end of the accounting year. In other words, the business has already provided the service or earned the income, but the payment is still receivable.

According to the Matching Principle of accounting, income should be recognized in the accounting period in which it is earned, irrespective of whether cash has actually been received. Therefore, accrued income is added to the income of the current year to determine the correct profit.

Questions relating to accrued income, adjustment entries, and treatment in final accounts are frequently asked in university examinations.

Examples of accrued income include interest accrued but not received, commission earned but not received, rent receivable, dividend receivable, and professional fees earned but outstanding.

For example, suppose a business earns commission of Rs. 10,000 during the year but receives only Rs. 8,000 before the end of the year. The remaining Rs. 2,000 is accrued income because it has been earned but not yet received.

The journal entry for recording accrued income is:

Accrued Income A/c Dr.  
  To Income A/c

For example:

Outstanding Interest A/c Dr.  
  To Interest Received A/c

In this entry, Accrued Income Account is debited because it represents an amount receivable and therefore becomes an asset. The Income Account is credited because income increases.

Accrued income affects final accounts in two places.

In the Profit and Loss Account, accrued income is added to the related income because it belongs to the current accounting period.

In the Balance Sheet, accrued income is shown on the assets side because it represents an amount receivable.

Example:

Commission received = Rs. 8,000  
Accrued commission = Rs. 2,000

In Profit and Loss Account:

Commission Income = Rs. 10,000

In Balance Sheet:

Accrued Commission = Rs. 2,000 (Asset)

This dual effect of adjustment is an important examination point.

Accrued income increases the income of the business and therefore increases net profit.

It is important to distinguish accrued income from income received in advance. Accrued income is earned but not yet received and is treated as an asset, whereas income received in advance is received before being earned and is treated as a liability.

Thus, Accrued Income represents income already earned but not yet collected and is adjusted to ensure proper determination of profit and accurate presentation of financial position. Proper understanding of accrued income is essential because it frequently appears in final accounts and university examinations.
